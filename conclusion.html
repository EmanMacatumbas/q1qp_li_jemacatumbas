Conclusion

We have deduced that there are a few trends that appear when looking at the data. According to the data, it is within the Afro-Eurasia region where we can find more than 75% of the countries from the sample. We can thus infer that this region contains a large number of countries that exhibit factors that cause depression rates to increase.

Another trend we have observed is that there are more “well-off” countries in the sample and depression seems to be a larger problem, or atleast is more prevalent, in countries of higher income level classes. This can be seen from the Qualitative variables I. & II. as well as Quantitative variable II. This was a surprise to us as we expected “worse-off” countries to be more common and have higher rates. This outcome could be due to a few reasons.

According to research, an increase in wealth does in fact correlate to an increase in psychological stress. This stress may derive from the pressure of high expectations and obligations towards one`s family and society. The article also confirms that “Research shows that wealthy countries do experience higher rates of depression, Marcum says.” It also states that “wealthier children of wealthy parents may have a higher chance of developing mental health conditions like depression and anxiety.” (Coelho, 2022).

Though this study only analyzes a small portion of countries and new trends may appear when studying the whole population, this study aims to assist future researchers to apply the results that we have found and formulate new studies to discover more ways in handling the issue at hand.
